The Basics

What Is a Non-Operated Working Interest?

A non-operated working interest (NOWI) is a direct ownership position in an oil and gas lease. As a working interest owner, you hold a real economic stake in the production from that lease — you share proportionately in both the costs and the revenues.

The "non-operated" designation means you are not the operator of record — you do not manage drilling rigs, hire crews, or handle day-to-day field operations. That responsibility belongs to the designated operator, typically an established E&P company with a proven track record.

Through Basin Ventures, you invest as a Limited Partner in a Texas Limited Partnership that holds working interests across multiple wells and operators. This structure gives you direct ownership economics with limited liability protection — your financial exposure is capped at your capital contribution.

Income Mechanics

How NOWI Income Works

Understanding the flow of capital from investment to production revenue.

1

Capital Deployment

Your investment funds your proportionate share of drilling and completion costs across the wells in your fund. The majority of these costs (85-90%) are classified as Intangible Drilling Costs (IDCs), deductible against active ordinary income in Year 1.

2

Drilling & Completion

The designated operator drills, completes, and brings the well(s) into production. Basin monitors every phase of operations, tracking progress, costs, and completion milestones on behalf of investors.

3

Production & Revenue

Once in production, oil and gas are sold at market prices. Revenue flows to the working interest owners proportionate to their ownership stake, net of operating expenses and royalties.

4

Distributions

Production revenue is distributed to investors on a regular schedule. Basin provides full transparency on production volumes, pricing, operating costs, and net revenue through the proprietary investor portal.

Tax Advantages

Why NOWI Is the Most Tax-Efficient Energy Investment

These benefits are exclusive to direct working interest participation and unavailable through public energy stocks, ETFs, mutual funds, or REITs.

85-90%

Intangible Drilling Costs (IDCs)

IDCs include labor, chemicals, mud, grease, and other non-salvageable costs associated with drilling. These typically represent 85-90% of total well costs and are generally deductible in the year the investment is made. For a $100,000 investment, approximately $85,000-$90,000 may be deductible against active ordinary income in Year 1.

100%

Tangible Drilling Costs (TDCs) & Bonus Depreciation

TDCs represent the physical, salvageable equipment — wellheads, casing, tubing, tanks. Under current law including the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, eligible TDCs may qualify for up to 100% bonus depreciation in Year 1. Combined with IDC deductions, this means up to 100% of your investment may be deductible in the year it is made.

15%

Percentage Depletion Allowance

15% of gross income from production is generally tax-free for small and independent producers. This is an ongoing annual benefit for the productive life of the well — not a one-time deduction. It applies regardless of your original cost basis, meaning you can receive tax-free income even after your investment has been fully recovered.

Active Income Exception — IRC §469(c)(3)

This is the key advantage. A working interest in an oil and gas drilling program is one of the very few investments explicitly exempt from passive activity loss limitations under the Internal Revenue Code. This means your IDC and TDC deductions can offset active ordinary income — including W-2 wages, business income, and professional earnings — not just passive income. This benefit is exclusive to working interests and does not apply to royalty interests, mineral interests, public energy stocks, or any fund-of-funds structure.

Unsuccessful Investment Protection

In the event of a non-producing well (dry hole), nearly 100% of invested capital may be written off as an ordinary loss against active taxable income. Compare this to stock market losses, where only $3,000/year can be deducted against ordinary income — the rest must offset capital gains. This downside protection makes the economic risk profile of NOWI fundamentally different from equity investing.
