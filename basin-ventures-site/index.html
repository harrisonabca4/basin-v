How It Works

Non-Operated Working Interest Investing

A non-operated working interest (NOWI) is a direct ownership stake in an oil and gas lease. You participate in the costs and revenues of production without managing day-to-day operations — Basin and its operator partners handle that.

1

We Source & Vet

Basin screens hundreds of opportunities annually through our four-pillar diligence process. Only the top fraction advance.

2

You Invest

As an accredited investor, you commit capital to a Basin fund and receive LP ownership in a Texas Limited Partnership.

3

Operators Drill & Produce

Established, top-tier E&P operators manage the physical drilling and production operations. You own the economics, not the headaches.

4

You Receive Distributions

Production revenue flows to you as the owner — with powerful tax benefits including IDC deductions, depletion allowance, and bonus depreciation.

Tax Advantages

Benefits Exclusive to Direct Participation

These deductions and allowances are unavailable through public energy stocks, ETFs, mutual funds, or REITs.

85-90%

Intangible Drilling Costs

Deduct 85-90% of your investment against active ordinary income — including W-2 wages — in the year the investment is made.

100%

Bonus Depreciation (TDC)

Tangible drilling costs may qualify for 100% bonus depreciation in Year 1 under current law including the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.

15%

Depletion Allowance

15% of gross production income is generally tax-free for small and independent producers — an ongoing benefit for the life of the asset.

Active Income Exception — IRC §469(c)(3)

A working interest in an oil and gas program is explicitly exempt from passive activity loss limitations. IDC and TDC deductions can offset active ordinary income including W-2 wages and business income — unlike nearly every other tax shelter available.

Dry Hole Protection

In the unlikely event of a non-producing well, nearly 100% of invested capital may be written off as an ordinary loss against active taxable income — unlike stock losses, which are capped at $3,000/year against ordinary income.
